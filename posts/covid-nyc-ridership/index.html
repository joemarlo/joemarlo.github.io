Inequities in the fall of NYC subway ridership

Mapping the decline of subway ridership in the Covid era

[This post is still in draft]

Ridership on New York City’s subway system has declined significantly amid the Coronavirus pandemic. The Metropolitan Transportation Authority (MTA), which runs the subway, is in the midst of a fiscal crisis — reportedly facing a $16 billion deficit due to the decline.

It’s easy to see the severity of the situation after visualizing the daily ridership over 2020. Over the course of one month, which included a New York statewide stay-at-home order, the MTA witnessed an unprecedented fall in straphangers. But is the decline in ridership spread equally across NYC’s diverse residents and neighborhoods?


Putting finite numbers on this fall, we can take the difference in means between the period before the precipice and once ridership flattens out: an 88% mean decline in population level ridership.


Extending this basic methodology to each subway station reveals a distinct pattern playing out across the boroughs. Manhattan — home to the wealthy and the transient — saw the largest declines in ridership while the outer-boroughs — especially the Bronx — still saw large but relatively the smallest declines. A Voronoi diagram (below left) is overlaid on the map to estimate the encompassing neighborhoods around a given subway station. This provides a reasonable approximation of the persons who are likely trafficking the station.

To dig deeper into understanding who these persons are, projecting the points on to the Census Public Use Microdata Areas (PUMA, below right) allows us to tie the ridership to demographic variables that the Census tracks through the American Community Survey (ACS) such as income, age, job industry, and ….


Regions mapped by proximity to subway station


Regions mapped by Census PUMA code

The first question to be addressed: are smaller decreases in ridership explained by essential worker status? Essential worker status can be approximated (below left) based on the industry residents labor in. Aligning these industries to the Delaware essential industry list (one of the few discrete lists available), the proportion of essential workers can be estimated. It’s difficult to verify the accuracy but it seems roughly in line with the US estimate from the Economic Policy Institute which claims 55 million American workers are essential.

Second, how does ridership correlate with income? Below right.

The income story is right in line with expectations with Manhattan and the northern parts of Brooklyn clearly representing the wealthiest neighborhoods. The essential worker map is a mixed bag; parts of Brooklyn and Manhattan are much more essential that expected. And some are much less essential than expected such as East Williamsburg and Bushwick in Brooklyn and Manhattan near Columbia University. However, the Bronx and south Brooklyn are more essential which aligns with the ridership map.
